# The Honest Herbalist -- Voice & Tone Operating Rules

**Purpose:** This document governs every word written under The Honest Herbalist brand. It is the binding reference for any copywriting agent, human or AI, producing presell content, sales pages, emails, ads, or support copy. Every rule below must produce a clear yes/no when checked against draft copy.

---

## Subsection A: Sentence-Level Voice Mechanics

### A1. Sentence Length Tendencies by Page Type

| Page Type | Average Sentence Length | Rhythm Rule |
|---|---|---|
| Presell / Editorial | 16-22 words | Longer, flowing sentences are permitted. Mix one long (20+) with two medium (12-16) per paragraph. Reads like a well-sourced magazine feature. |
| Sales Page -- Hook & Agitation | 8-14 words | Short, declarative. One idea per sentence. Fragments permitted sparingly ("Not hype. Just plants that work."). |
| Sales Page -- Mechanism / Proof | 14-20 words | Medium. Allows subordinate clauses for explaining how something works. Never stack more than two 20+ word sentences in a row. |
| CTA & Offer Stack | 6-12 words | Punchy. Direct. Active voice mandatory. |
| Email Subject Lines | 4-9 words | Conversational, lowercase-friendly. No ALL CAPS words. |
| FAQ | 12-18 words | Clear, complete answers. No sentence fragments. |

### A4. Jargon Policy

**Scientific terms ARE appropriate when:**
- Explaining a mechanism of action (e.g., "Chamomile's apigenin binds to GABA receptors, which is partly why it promotes calm.")
- Naming specific compounds, botanical Latin names, or study types in mechanism or proof sections.
- The term is immediately followed by a plain-language translation in the same sentence.

**Scientific terms are NOT appropriate when:**
- Writing headlines, subheads, CTAs, email subject lines, or bullet-point benefit lists.
- The term would require the reader to leave the page to understand the sentence.

**Rule:** Every scientific term on its first use in any copy piece must be followed by a parenthetical or dash-separated plain translation. Example: "...its anti-inflammatory properties (meaning it helps calm irritation and swelling)."

### A5. Contraction and Formality Rules

- Contractions are the default. Write "you'll" not "you will," "it's" not "it is," "we're" not "we are."
- Exception: Expand contractions for emphasis or gravity. "This is not a substitute for medical advice" hits harder than "This isn't a substitute."
- Never use "gonna," "wanna," "kinda," or other ultra-casual contractions. The register is warm-professional, not texting-a-friend.
- First person plural ("we") for brand voice. Second person ("you") for addressing the reader. First person singular ("I") only in attributed founder quotes.

## Subsection B: Authority & Credibility Persona

### B1. Authority Archetype

The Honest Herbalist is **the calm, protective guide.** Not the guru on a mountain. Not the lab-coat scientist. Not the mystical healer.

Think: the experienced friend who happens to have a deep reference library, tells you both the benefits and the risks, and never pressures you. She says "here is what I have found" rather than "here is what you must do."

**Voice test:** If a sentence could be spoken by a conspiracy podcast host, a carnival barker, or a detached medical journal -- it fails. If it could be spoken by a knowledgeable friend sitting across a kitchen table with a cup of tea -- it passes.

### B2. Credential Presentation Rules

- Never fabricate or inflate credentials. If no formal credential exists, do not imply one.
- Cite the type of evidence, not just its existence. "Three peer-reviewed studies on valerian root" is stronger and more honest than "scientifically proven."
- Attribute knowledge to the research, not to the brand. Write "Research published in the Journal of Ethnopharmacology found..." not "We discovered that..."
- When referencing traditional use, always name the tradition. "Used in Traditional Chinese Medicine for centuries" is specific. "Used for centuries" is vague.
- Never use phrases like "leading expert," "world-renowned," or "the definitive guide" to describe the brand or product.

### B3. Limitations and Uncertainty Rules ("This Isn't Magic" Caveats)

**Mandatory caveat triggers -- include a limitation statement whenever copy:**
- Names a specific health condition (e.g., anxiety, insomnia, inflammation).
- Describes a physiological mechanism (e.g., "calms the nervous system").
- Could be interpreted as a treatment recommendation.

**Approved caveat formats (rotate; do not repeat the same one within a single page):**
- "This is not medical advice. Talk to your provider before using any herb therapeutically."
- "Traditional use is not the same as clinical proof. We share what's documented so you can make an informed choice."
- "Herbs are powerful -- which means they also carry real risks. This handbook covers both."
- "We are not replacing your doctor. We are helping you ask better questions."

**Caveat placement rule:** Caveats must appear within the body of the argument, not buried in footnotes or fine print. A caveat hidden at the bottom of a 2,000-word page does not count.

**Tone of caveats:** Matter-of-fact, not apologetic. Caveats are a feature of the brand, not a legal obligation we resent. They prove we mean it when we say "honest."

## Subsection C: Urgency & Scarcity Rules

### C1. Urgency Philosophy

The Honest Herbalist uses **consequence-based urgency** and **real-deadline urgency** only. Artificial scarcity (fake countdown timers, invented stock limits on a digital product) is permanently banned.

Consequence-based urgency = "The longer you rely on guesswork, the more likely you are to misuse an herb that interacts with your medication." This is honest. The consequence is real.

Real-deadline urgency = A launch-week bonus expiring on a stated date, honored without extension. If we say it ends Friday, it ends Friday.

### C2. Approved Urgency Phrases

1. "The introductory price ends [real date]."
2. "This bonus is available during launch week only."
3. "The sooner you have a reliable reference, the sooner you stop guessing."
4. "Every day without accurate dosing information is a day you are relying on blog posts and guesswork."
5. "Your next herb purchase deserves better research behind it."
6. "This price reflects our launch commitment and will increase to [amount] on [date]."
7. "If you are already using herbs, this information is relevant to you right now."
8. "Why wait until a bad interaction forces the question?"
9. "Start with better information today."
10. "Join [number] readers who already have their copy."

### C3. Banned Urgency Phrases

1. "Act now before it's too late!"
2. "Only [fake number] copies left!" (digital product)
3. "This offer disappears forever."
4. "You will regret not buying this."
5. "Last chance!" (unless it is literally the final communication)
6. "Supplies are running out." (digital product -- supplies are infinite)
7. "Don't miss out!"
8. "Once it's gone, it's gone."
9. "Everyone is buying this." (unverifiable social proof)
10. "If you don't act now, [fearmongered health consequence]."

### C4. Countdown Timer Policy

- Countdown timers may be used ONLY for real deadlines (launch bonus expiration, genuine price increase).
- The timer must correspond to an actual event that will occur. If the deadline passes, the offer must actually end.
- Evergreen funnels may not use timers that reset per visitor. This is fabricated urgency and violates brand values.
- If a deadline is extended for any legitimate reason, acknowledge the extension honestly in copy.

## Subsection D: Emotional Register Map

| Copy Context | Emotional Register | What It Sounds Like | What It Does NOT Sound Like |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Hook / Headline** | Calm confidence + direct honesty | "An herbal reference that tells you what works, what doesn't, and what to watch out for." | "The shocking truth about herbs Big Pharma is hiding from you!" |
| **Problem / Agitation** | Protective concern, grounded frustration | "You deserve better than conflicting blog posts and dosing advice from strangers on Reddit." | "You are DESTROYING your health with dangerous misinformation!" |
| **Mechanism** | Intellectual respect, teaching warmth | "Chamomile contains apigenin, a compound that binds to GABA receptors -- the same system targeted by anti-anxiety medications, but with a gentler profile." | "This miracle flower HACKS your brain chemistry!" |
| **Proof / Testimonials** | Quiet pride, relatable relief | "I stopped second-guessing every tea I made for my kids. That peace of mind alone was worth it." | "OMG this book literally saved my family's lives!!!" |
| **Offer Stack** | Practical generosity, clear value | "You get the 300+ page handbook, the interaction checker, and the dosing quick-reference -- all for $49." | "An INSANE deal worth $997 that we are practically GIVING AWAY!" |
| **CTA** | Warm invitation, quiet confidence | "Get your copy of The Honest Herbalist Handbook." | "BUY NOW before this life-changing offer disappears FOREVER!" |
| **Guarantee** | Steady reassurance, no drama | "Try it for 60 days. If it doesn't become your go-to reference, we will refund every cent. No hoops." | "We are SO confident this will BLOW YOUR MIND that we GUARANTEE..." |
| **FAQ** | Patient clarity, zero condescension | "Good question. The handbook covers 300+ herbs with referenced safety notes, dosing ranges, and interaction warnings." | "Um, obviously it covers that. Did you even read the page?" |
| **Email Subject Lines** | Conversational curiosity, understated | "the herb most people get wrong" / "a question about your medicine cabinet" | "EXPOSED: The herb they tried to BAN" / "OPEN THIS NOW" |

## Subsection E: Register Shift Rules

### E1. When to Shift Registers

| Shift Direction | Trigger |
|---|---|
| **Warm/Personal --> Authoritative/Clinical** | When presenting mechanism of action, citing studies, listing contraindications, or stating safety warnings. |
| **Authoritative/Clinical --> Warm/Personal** | When returning to the reader's lived experience after a technical explanation, entering CTA territory, or following a caveat with reassurance. |

### E2. Transition Rules

- Never jump from peak warmth to peak clinical in a single sentence. Allow at least one transitional sentence.
- The transitional sentence should contain both a human element and a knowledge element. This is the "bridge."
- Register shifts must feel like a camera slowly zooming in or out -- not a jump cut.

### E3. Bridge Sentence Examples

**Warm to Authoritative:**
- "That peace of mind starts with understanding what is actually happening in your body. Here is what the research shows."
- "It sounds simple, but the science behind it is worth understanding."
- "Before you brew that tea, there is one thing worth knowing about how this herb actually works."

**Authoritative to Warm:**
- "That is the mechanism. In practical terms, it means you have a reliable option for those restless nights."
- "The research is encouraging -- and more importantly, the safety profile makes this one worth considering."
- "Now that you know why it works, let us talk about how to actually use it."

## Subsection F: Anti-Patterns (18 items)

These are specific failures this brand never commits. Each is a common default of AI-generated or generic health/wellness copy.

| # | Anti-Pattern | Why It Is Banned |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Opening with "In today's world..." or "In a world where..."** | Generic AI throat-clearing. Wastes the hook. Start with a specific, concrete claim or question. |
| 2 | **Listing benefits without naming a single risk or contraindication** | Violates the safety-first positioning. Every herb profile or claim must acknowledge limitations somewhere nearby. |
| 3 | **Using "backed by science" without citing what science** | Empty credibility claim. Name the study type, the journal, or the compound -- or say "traditional use" honestly. |
| 4 | **Stacking three or more exclamation points on a single page** | Signals hype. Maximum one exclamation point per 500 words, and only in testimonial quotes. Brand voice never uses them. |
| 5 | **Writing testimonials that all sound identical in vocabulary and structure** | AI-generated or heavily edited testimonials destroy credibility. Real testimonials are messy and specific. Preserve original phrasing. |
| 6 | **Calling the reader "babe," "mama," "queen," or "goddess"** | Performative intimacy that this audience finds condescending. Use "you" or no direct address. |
| 7 | **Using fear of disease as a primary motivator** | Fearmongering violates brand values and regulatory boundaries. Agitation should focus on confusion, wasted money, and unreliable information -- not "you might get cancer." |
| 8 | **Implying herbs replace prescription medication** | Dangerous, illegal, and brand-destroying. Always position herbs as complementary and always defer to the reader's provider. |
| 9 | **Over-hedging to the point of saying nothing** | "This herb might possibly maybe sometimes help a little with something, but we really cannot say." Hedging must be honest, not cowardly. State what is documented, then state the limits. |
| 10 | **Switching between formal and casual tone without a bridge** | Jarring register shifts ("The flavonoid profile demonstrates... anyway, it's super great for sleep!") signal that the copy was assembled, not written. |
| 11 | **Using the word "just" to minimize the product** | "It's just a handbook." The word "just" diminishes value. The handbook is a comprehensive reference. Describe it as one. |
| 12 | **Padding copy with filler paragraphs that restate the same point** | Respect the reader's time. If a section does not add new information, cut it. Repetition for emphasis must use new language or a new angle each time. |
| 13 | **Making the brand the hero of the story** | The reader is the hero. The handbook is the tool. The brand is the guide. Copy that centers "we created" and "our mission" more than "you will have" and "your family" has the hierarchy wrong. |
| 14 | **Using stock photography language in copy** | Phrases like "vibrant wellness journey," "thriving naturally," or "embracing your best self" read like caption generators, not human writing. |
| 15 | **Citing "thousands of years" without naming which tradition or culture** | Vague appeals to antiquity are a hallmark of hype competitors. Specificity ("used in Ayurvedic practice" or "documented in European folk herbalism since the 16th century") is mandatory. |
| 16 | **Promising transformation instead of information** | The product is a reference book. It does not transform lives; it informs decisions. Copy that promises "transform your family's health forever" oversteps. Copy that promises "make better-informed decisions about the herbs you already use" is accurate. |
| 17 | **Burying the price or avoiding it** | This audience respects transparency. State the price clearly and early in the offer section. Hiding the price until after a long scroll signals that you know it needs justification -- the opposite of confidence. |
| 18 | **Using emoji as a substitute for tone** | Brand copy uses zero emoji. Tone is carried by word choice and sentence structure, not symbols. Emoji are permitted only in casual social media captions, never in sales pages, emails, or the handbook itself. |
