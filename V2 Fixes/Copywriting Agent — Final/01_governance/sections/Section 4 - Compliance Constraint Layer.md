## SUBSECTION B: Category-Specific Language Restrictions

### 1. FDA Claim Hierarchy (Applied to Marketing an Informational Product About Herbs)

Even though this is a book and not a supplement, the FTC evaluates the "net impression" of advertising on the consumer. If an ad for an herbal reference book implies readers will achieve health outcomes, the FTC treats those implied claims the same as direct product claims.

**Structure/Function Claims -- USABLE WITH CARE:**
These describe how a nutrient or ingredient affects the normal structure or function of the body. The handbook's marketing may reference these as educational content the book covers.
- Acceptable framing: "Learn how herbalists have traditionally used chamomile to support relaxation"
- Acceptable framing: "Discover which herbs are studied for their role in supporting immune function"
- The book itself does not need to carry the FDA disclaimer ("This statement has not been evaluated...") because it is not a supplement label, but ad copy should not make claims that would require such a disclaimer if the product were a supplement.

**Health Claims -- HANDLE WITH EXTREME CAUTION:**
These describe a relationship between a substance and reduced risk of disease. FDA must authorize these claims for supplement labeling. In ad copy for an informational product, avoid implying that using the book's information will reduce disease risk.
- Problematic: "This handbook shows you how to reduce your risk of heart disease with herbs"
- Safer: "Explore the research behind herbs studied in the context of cardiovascular wellness"

**Disease Claims -- PROHIBITED IN ALL MARKETING COPY:**
Any claim that a product (or, by strong implication, the information in a product) can diagnose, cure, mitigate, treat, or prevent a disease is a drug claim under FDA rules and a deceptive claim under FTC rules if unsubstantiated.
- Prohibited even in educational framing: "Learn how to treat your arthritis naturally"
- Prohibited: "The herbal cures for Type 2 diabetes your doctor won't tell you about"

*Source: [FDA Structure/Function Claims Compliance Guide](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/small-entity-compliance-guide-structurefunction-claims); [FTC Health Products Compliance Guidance](https://www.ftc.gov/business-guidance/resources/health-products-compliance-guidance)*

### 2. FTC Guidelines for Marketing Educational Health Products

The FTC's Health Products Compliance Guidance (updated December 2022) applies to ALL health-related products, including informational products. Key principles:

- **Net Impression Standard:** The FTC evaluates the overall impression an ad conveys to a reasonable consumer, not just individual words. If the totality of an ad for a book about herbal remedies implies the consumer will achieve health outcomes by reading it, that implication is a health claim requiring substantiation.
- **Third-Party Literature Rule:** The FTC does not regulate statements in independently written books, BUT advertisers cannot deceptively use those statements in ads. Quoting a passage from the handbook in an ad that implies a health outcome makes the advertiser liable for that implied claim.
- **Substantiation Before Dissemination:** All health benefit claims in advertising must be substantiated BEFORE the ad runs. "Competent and reliable scientific evidence" generally means randomized controlled human clinical trials. Animal studies and in vitro research alone are insufficient.

*Source: [FTC Health Claims](https://www.ftc.gov/business-guidance/advertising-marketing/health-claims); [FTC Advertising and Marketing Basics](https://www.ftc.gov/business-guidance/advertising-marketing/advertising-marketing-basics)*

### 3. Testimonial Rules

Under the FTC's Endorsement Guides (revised 2023, current as of 2026):

- **"Results not typical" disclaimers are insufficient.** The FTC tested these and found they do not reduce consumer perception that results are representative. If a testimonial reports dramatic results, the ad must disclose what results a typical consumer can actually expect.
- **Two-option rule:** Either (a) have proof the testimonial results are typical, or (b) clearly and conspicuously disclose generally expected performance.
- **Scientific substantiation required:** Testimonials cannot be used to make health claims the advertiser could not substantiate directly. A reader saying "This book helped me manage my blood pressure" in an ad implies the book's content achieves that outcome -- requiring the same substantiation as a direct claim.
- **Material connections must be disclosed:** Any relationship between endorser and seller (free copy, affiliate commission, payment) must be clearly disclosed.

*Source: [FTC Endorsement Guides FAQ](https://www.ftc.gov/business-guidance/resources/ftcs-endorsement-guides-what-people-are-asking); [16 CFR Part 255](https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-16/chapter-I/subchapter-B/part-255)*

### 4. UK ASA/CAP Rules for Health Marketing Claims

For UK-targeted advertising, the Advertising Standards Authority (ASA) and Committee of Advertising Practice (CAP) Code apply:

- **Food supplements cannot claim to treat disease.** This is a strict prohibition under CAP Code Section 15 and the UK Nutrition and Health Claims Regulation (NHCR). Only health claims listed on the Great Britain Nutrition and Health Claims Register are permitted.
- **Herbal medicines require Traditional Herbal Registration (THR)** to make medicinal claims. Marketing an informational book does not require THR, but ad copy cannot make medicinal claims about herbs unless the specific herb holds that registration.
- **"Based on traditional use only"** -- even registered THMs must include this qualifier and must not imply clinical trial evidence.
- **ASA AI Active Ad Monitoring** is scanning online ads proactively in 2025-2026. Enforcement is at its highest level, with multiple rulings against supplement advertisers in late 2025.
- **No health professional endorsements** for medicines in ads. For food/supplement claims, ads cannot reference recommendations of individual health professionals.

*Source: [ASA Health: Herbal Medicine](https://www.asa.org.uk/advice-online/health-herbal-medicine.html); [CAP Code Section 12: Medicines, Treatments, Devices and Health](https://www.asa.org.uk/static/uploaded/4c8e6b31-c6a5-40a0-9ac0b2924d8297ca.pdf); [CAP Code Section 15: Food, Food Supplements and Health Claims](https://www.asa.org.uk/type/non_broadcast/code_section/15.html)*

## SUBSECTION E: Disclaimer Requirements and Placement

### 1. Required Disclaimers

**A. Educational Health Content Marketing Disclaimer**
Use on all advertising assets (ads, landing pages, emails):

> "The Honest Herbalist Handbook is an educational reference. It is not intended to provide medical advice, diagnosis, or treatment. The information in this handbook is not a substitute for professional medical advice. Always consult a qualified healthcare provider before making changes to your health regimen."

**B. Testimonial Disclaimer (When Using Reader Testimonials)**
Must accompany every testimonial that references a health-related experience:

> "Individual experiences shared are personal and not representative of typical results. This handbook is an educational resource; individual outcomes from applying any information will vary. Consult a healthcare professional regarding your specific health needs."

Per FTC guidelines, if a testimonial implies results that exceed what is generally expected, you must also disclose what results are typical. "Results not typical" alone is NOT sufficient.

**C. Affiliate/Sponsorship Disclosure**
Required whenever a paid or material relationship exists between endorser and seller:

> "This post contains affiliate links. If you purchase through these links, [Name/Publication] may receive a commission at no additional cost to you."

For influencer/creator partnerships, the disclosure must appear:
- Meta/Instagram: In the first line of caption text (not buried below "more"); use platform's Paid Partnership tag
- TikTok: In video itself (spoken or on-screen text in first 3 seconds); use TikTok's branded content toggle
- YouTube: Use YouTube's paid promotion checkbox; verbal disclosure in first 30 seconds of video
- Email: In the header area, clearly labeled

*Source: [FTC Endorsement Guides FAQ](https://www.ftc.gov/business-guidance/resources/ftcs-endorsement-guides-what-people-are-asking)*

**D. Email Marketing Disclaimers**
Every marketing email must include:

> [Clear identification that this is an advertisement or marketing communication]
> [Sender's valid physical postal address]
> [Functional unsubscribe link with clear instructions]

For UK recipients, additional PECR/GDPR requirements apply: recipients must have opted in (consent-based model, not opt-out).

### 2. Placement Rules

| Disclaimer Type | Placement Requirement | Platform Notes |
|----------------|----------------------|----------------|
| Educational/not-medical-advice | On landing page: above the fold or immediately following any health-related claim. In ads: within the ad copy itself (not only on landing page). | Meta requires landing page consistency with ad claims. Google evaluates full landing page. |
| Testimonial disclaimer | Immediately adjacent to the testimonial -- same visual field, same font size minimum (no fine print at page bottom) | FTC requires "clear and conspicuous" -- must be noticeable to ordinary consumers without searching for it |
| Affiliate disclosure | Before the first affiliate link or commercial recommendation | FTC and ASA both require disclosure before the consumer encounters the commercial content |
| Email CAN-SPAM elements | Physical address and unsubscribe: in email footer. Ad identification: in header or opening section. | Must be functional for 30 days post-send; opt-out honored within 10 business days |

### 3. Platform-Specific Disclaimer Requirements

**Meta:** Landing page must not contain claims that contradict or exceed ad copy. Health disclaimers should appear in ad text, not just on landing pages. Testimonials in ads need disclaimers within the ad unit.

**TikTok:** Branded content toggle must be enabled for all paid partnerships. Health disclaimers should appear as on-screen text or in the video caption. Verbal disclaimers in video content are recommended but text is required.

**YouTube/Google Ads:** Paid promotion checkbox must be activated. Landing page health disclaimers are evaluated during ad review. False or exaggerated claims on landing pages trigger ad disapproval even if the ad copy itself is compliant.

**Email:** CAN-SPAM physical address and unsubscribe are legally mandatory. UK PECR requires prior consent for marketing emails. ESP-specific rules may add further restrictions (e.g., Mailchimp prohibits certain health supplement categories).
