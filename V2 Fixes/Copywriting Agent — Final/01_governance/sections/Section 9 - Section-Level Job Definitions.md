# Section 9: Section-Level Job Definitions with Success Criteria

## Operational Specification for The Honest Herbalist Handbook Copywriting Agent

**Brand context:** The Honest Herbalist Handbook | $49 digital herbal reference | DTC health/wellness
**Audience:** Women 25-55, safety-conscious, anti-hype, "crunchy but not anti-science," Stage 5 market sophistication
**Companion docs:** Subsection A (Structural Principles), Section 2 (Page-Type Templates), Section 3 (Voice & Tone), Section 5 (Awareness-Level Routing Logic)

**Governing principle:** Every section of copy has exactly one job. If a section cannot state its job in a single sentence, it is doing too many things. If the reader's mental state after reading does not match the defined success state, the section has failed regardless of how well the prose reads.

## SUBSECTION A: SECTION JOB CARDS

---

### CARD 1

```
SECTION TYPE: Hero Section (Headline + Subhead + Above-Fold)
JOB (one sentence): Stop the scroll, identify the reader, and make her believe she is in
the right place within 5 seconds.
SUCCESS STATE: "This is for someone exactly like me. I want to keep reading."
FAILURE STATE: "This is another hyped-up wellness product" OR "This is too clinical /
not for me" OR "I have no idea what this is."
MEASURABLE PROXY: Scroll-to-second-section rate (target: 65%+); bounce rate within
first 10 seconds (target: under 40%).

COMMON FAILURE MODES:
  1. Hype leakage in the headline. A single banned-list word ("miracle," "secret,"
     "revolutionary") in the hero triggers instant categorization as the same wellness
     noise she is trying to escape. Prevention: Run the headline against the Section 3
     banned-word list before finalizing. Every word.
  2. Cleverness over clarity. A headline that prioritizes wordplay over immediate
     comprehension fails the 5-second test. This audience rewards directness, not
     intrigue. Prevention: Apply the "can a stranger understand what this product is
     and who it is for within one read?" test. If no, rewrite for clarity.
  3. Missing identity signal. The headline names the product but not the reader. She
     needs to see herself -- a safety-conscious woman navigating herbal decisions --
     reflected in the first screen. Prevention: Confirm the subhead or supporting line
     contains at least one audience-identity marker (e.g., "for women who want natural
     remedies without the guesswork").

SELF-EVALUATION CHECKLIST:
  [ ] Does the headline pass the 5-second clarity test -- could a stranger identify what
      the product is and who it serves?
  [ ] Is the headline free of every word on the Section 3 banned list?
  [ ] Does the above-fold copy contain at least one identity marker that lets the target
      reader see herself?
  [ ] Does the subhead advance a specific benefit or desired outcome (not just restate
      the headline in different words)?
  [ ] Is the emotional register "calm confidence" -- not excitement, not urgency, not
      clinical detachment?
  [ ] Does the hero avoid making a claim that requires proof not yet provided on the page?
```

### CARD 2

```
SECTION TYPE: Problem / Agitation Section
JOB (one sentence): Name the reader's frustration with herbal information so precisely
that she feels understood, then externalize the blame to the ecosystem so she feels
relieved rather than shamed.
SUCCESS STATE: "Finally, someone who gets it. It is not my fault the information out
there is terrible."
FAILURE STATE: "This is guilting me" OR "This is exaggerating to scare me into buying"
OR "I already know this -- tell me something new."
MEASURABLE PROXY: Scroll-through rate past the agitation section without bouncing
(target: 80%+); time-on-section indicating reading rather than skimming (target: 15-25
seconds for 150-200 words).

COMMON FAILURE MODES:
  1. Agitation exceeds Level 3 on the calibration scale. This is the single highest-risk
     failure mode for this brand. Any sentence that could be read as fearmongering about
     child safety, catastrophic health outcomes, or parental negligence will cause
     immediate disengagement. Prevention: After drafting, score every agitation sentence
     against the Subsection A scale. Any sentence at Level 4+ must be deleted or
     rewritten to Level 3 unless it is in the presell advertorial's single permitted
     Level 4 instance.
  2. Missing the externalization beat. Agitation without system-blame produces shame,
     not buying intent. The reader blames herself and disengages. Prevention: Enforce
     the three-beat structure (Agitate, Empathy + Externalize, Bridge) from Subsection A
     Section 3.3. No agitation block is complete without all three beats.
  3. Generic problem framing. "There is so much confusing information out there" is
     invisible because she has read it a hundred times. Prevention: Name specific,
     recognizable scenarios -- elderberry dosing contradictions, echinacea quality
     variance, conflicting advice in Facebook groups. The details create the "she is
     reading my mind" response.
  4. Stacked agitation without relief. Two or more consecutive agitation paragraphs
     without an empathy beat or value signal reads as manipulation to this audience.
     Prevention: Insert an empathy or externalization statement every 2-3 sentences
     within any agitation block.

SELF-EVALUATION CHECKLIST:
  [ ] Does every agitation sentence score at Level 3 or below on the Subsection A
      calibration scale?
  [ ] Does the section follow the three-beat structure: Agitate, Empathy + Externalize,
      Bridge?
  [ ] Does the agitation name at least two specific, recognizable scenarios rather than
      generic frustration?
  [ ] Is the system (not the reader) blamed for the problem by the end of this section?
  [ ] Is there zero implication that the reader is a bad parent, lazy, or irresponsible?
  [ ] Does this section end with a forward-momentum sentence that bridges toward a
      solution, not a dead-end emotional low point?
  [ ] If the headline's DELIVERY_TEST (from its Promise Contract) is not yet fully
      satisfied by the Lead, does this section complete the delivery?
```

### CARD 3

```
SECTION TYPE: Mechanism Section
JOB (one sentence): Explain how The Honest System works in enough structural detail
that the reader could describe the differentiator to someone else in one sentence.
SUCCESS STATE: "This is genuinely different from every other herb book I have seen. I
understand why."
FAILURE STATE: "This is just a list of features" OR "I do not understand how this is
different from a Google search" OR "This sounds too complicated for me."
MEASURABLE PROXY: Scroll completion through mechanism section (target: 70%+); click-
through rate on CTA placed immediately after mechanism (target: higher than CTA after
problem section, indicating increased conviction).

COMMON FAILURE MODES:
  1. Feature list masquerading as mechanism. Listing "300+ herb profiles, safety flags,
     interaction checker" without explaining WHY each exists and what problem it solves
     reads as a spec sheet, not a reason to buy. Prevention: Every feature must be
     preceded by the problem it solves and followed by the outcome it creates. Structure:
     Problem-Feature-Outcome, not Feature-Feature-Feature.
  2. Mechanism too abstract. Describing "a safety-first system" without showing a
     concrete example of how the system works in practice. Prevention: Include at least
     one walkthrough -- "You look up chamomile. You see three green flags and one amber
     flag for drug interactions. The amber flag tells you exactly what to ask your
     doctor."
  3. Overclaiming the mechanism's novelty. Saying "no other resource does this" when the
     reader may own herb books with safety notes. Prevention: Claim structural
     differentiation ("the only reference that pairs every herb with interaction flags
     AND confidence-level ratings"), not categorical uniqueness.
  4. Excluding the reader. Technical language without plain-English translations makes
     the system feel like it is built for professionals. Prevention: Apply the Section 3
     jargon rule -- every technical term on first use must include a parenthetical or
     dash-separated plain translation.

SELF-EVALUATION CHECKLIST:
  [ ] Could the reader explain the mechanism's differentiator in one sentence after
      reading this section?
  [ ] Does every feature named include both the problem it solves and the outcome it
      produces?
  [ ] Is there at least one concrete walkthrough showing the system in action with a
      specific herb?
  [ ] Are all technical terms translated into plain language on first use?
  [ ] Does the section advance Belief 5 (this handbook is meaningfully different) without
      making unverifiable superiority claims?
  [ ] Does the tone remain "intellectual respect, teaching warmth" per the Section 3
      emotional register map?
```

### CARD 4

```
SECTION TYPE: Proof / Social Proof Section
JOB (one sentence): Reduce skepticism by providing specific, scenario-based evidence
from real users that the system works for women like the reader.
SUCCESS STATE: "Real people like me are using this and it is helping them make safer
decisions."
FAILURE STATE: "These testimonials sound fake" OR "This is generic praise that could
apply to any product" OR "None of these people are like me."
MEASURABLE PROXY: Scroll-through rate (target: 75%+); engagement with expandable
testimonials if used (click-to-expand rate target: 15%+).

COMMON FAILURE MODES:
  1. Generic testimonials. "Great book! Highly recommend!" provides zero evidence.
     Prevention: Every testimonial must name a specific herb, situation, or decision.
     At least one testimonial must reference a safety-related outcome (e.g., catching
     an interaction, avoiding a contraindicated herb).
  2. Testimonials that all sound identical. Same vocabulary, same sentence structure,
     same length -- signals fabrication to a Stage 5 sophisticated audience. Prevention:
     Preserve original phrasing and natural variation. If editing for clarity, retain the
     speaker's distinct voice.
  3. No identity match. All testimonials from demographics the reader does not recognize
     as her own. Prevention: Include at least one testimonial from a mother making
     family herbal decisions, at least one from a woman managing her own wellness, and
     ideally one "skeptic-converted" voice.
  4. Proof overkill. Stacking 8+ testimonials creates diminishing returns and feels
     desperate. Prevention: Limit to 3-5 testimonials on the sales page. Select for
     scenario diversity, not volume.

SELF-EVALUATION CHECKLIST:
  [ ] Does every testimonial reference a specific herb, scenario, or decision?
  [ ] Does at least one testimonial describe a safety-related outcome?
  [ ] Do the testimonials vary in voice, length, and vocabulary?
  [ ] Is at least one testimonial from a reader whose demographic profile matches the
      core avatar (mother, 25-55, natural-leaning)?
  [ ] Is the total count between 3 and 5 testimonials (not fewer, not more)?
  [ ] Are testimonials free of banned-list language and hype-adjacent phrasing?
```

### CARD 5

```
SECTION TYPE: Offer Stack Section
JOB (one sentence): Present everything the reader receives for $49 in a way that makes
the price feel like an obvious bargain without resorting to inflated value math.
SUCCESS STATE: "$49 is cheap for everything I am getting. This is clearly worth more
than that."
FAILURE STATE: "This fake $997 value is insulting my intelligence" OR "I still do not
know exactly what I am getting" OR "Why are there so many bonuses? What is the real
product?"
MEASURABLE PROXY: CTA click-through rate immediately following the offer stack (target:
highest CTA conversion rate on the page); scroll-past-without-clicking rate (target:
under 35%).

COMMON FAILURE MODES:
  1. Inflated value anchoring. "$10,000 worth of herbal wisdom for just $49!" is the
     fastest way to lose this audience. They recognize and despise this tactic.
     Prevention: Anchor against real, verifiable costs -- one naturopath consultation
     ($150+), one herbalism course ($200+), the time cost of researching 300+ herbs
     independently. Never assign a dollar value to individual components that the reader
     would find implausible.
  2. Bonus overload. More than 3-4 bonuses signals value-padding. The reader starts
     questioning the core product quality. Prevention: Cap bonuses at 4. Each bonus
     must solve a specific, named problem. No filler.
  3. Unclear deliverable format. The reader does not know if this is a PDF, a website,
     a physical book, or an app. Prevention: State the format explicitly in the first
     line of the offer stack (e.g., "Instant PDF download -- printable, searchable,
     yours forever").
  4. Price buried or obscured. Hiding the price at the bottom of a scrollable list
     signals shame. Prevention: State "$49" clearly and confidently alongside the
     deliverables. Price visibility is a trust signal for this audience.

SELF-EVALUATION CHECKLIST:
  [ ] Are all value anchors based on real, verifiable reference points (naturopath
      visits, course costs, research time) rather than inflated internal valuations?
  [ ] Is the deliverable format stated explicitly within the first two lines?
  [ ] Is the price ($49) stated clearly and visibly, not buried or de-emphasized?
  [ ] Does each bonus solve a named, specific problem (not generic "bonus value")?
  [ ] Is the total bonus count 4 or fewer?
  [ ] Does the tone remain "practical generosity" -- not carnival-barker stacking?
```

### CARD 6

```
SECTION TYPE: Risk Reversal / Guarantee Section
JOB (one sentence): Eliminate the last purchase barrier by framing the guarantee around
immediate, verifiable validation so the reader feels the risk is entirely on the seller.
SUCCESS STATE: "I can verify whether this is good within minutes of downloading it. If
it is not, I get my money back. There is no risk."
FAILURE STATE: "The guarantee sounds too good -- what is the catch?" OR "I do not
actually know how to tell if this is worth keeping" OR "Standard refund policy, nothing
special here."
MEASURABLE PROXY: CTA conversion rate after guarantee section versus before (target:
measurable lift); refund request rate (target: below 5%, indicating the guarantee
attracts rather than creates refunders).

COMMON FAILURE MODES:
  1. Guarantee without a validation prompt. "30-day money-back guarantee" is standard
     and invisible. Prevention: Frame the guarantee around a specific, immediate test:
     "Open the handbook. Look up one herb you already use. Check the safety flags. If
     it does not tell you something you did not already know, email us for a full
     refund." The test makes the guarantee feel earned, not reckless.
  2. Overblown guarantee language. "We are SO confident this will CHANGE YOUR LIFE..."
     violates the voice on every level. Prevention: Use steady, understated reassurance.
     The Section 3 emotional register for guarantees is "steady reassurance, no drama."
  3. Fine-print hedging that contradicts the headline promise. Saying "full refund" then
     listing conditions that make it difficult. Prevention: If conditions exist, state
     them plainly and briefly in the same section. No asterisks, no footnotes, no
     separate terms page for core guarantee terms.

SELF-EVALUATION CHECKLIST:
  [ ] Does the guarantee include a specific, immediate validation test the reader can
      perform within minutes of downloading?
  [ ] Is the tone "steady reassurance" -- free of exclamation points, hype language,
      and overconfidence signals?
  [ ] Are all guarantee conditions stated plainly in the same section (no fine-print
      redirects)?
  [ ] Does the guarantee frame the risk as sitting entirely with the seller, not the
      buyer?
  [ ] Is the guarantee positioned immediately before or adjacent to the primary CTA?
  [ ] Does the guarantee reinforce (not undermine) the brand's honesty positioning?
```

### CARD 7

```
SECTION TYPE: FAQ / Objection Handling Section
JOB (one sentence): Neutralize the 3-5 objections keeping fence-sitters from buying by
answering each one with a response that closes, not just informs.
SUCCESS STATE: "She anticipated my exact concern and gave me a real answer. I have no
remaining reason not to buy."
FAILURE STATE: "These are softball questions nobody actually asks" OR "The answers are
evasive" OR "Reading this raised objections I did not have before."
MEASURABLE PROXY: Scroll-through completion of FAQ (target: 60%+); CTA conversion
after FAQ section (target: measurable lift over mid-page CTA, indicating objection
resolution).

COMMON FAILURE MODES:
  1. Softball questions. "Why is The Honest Herbalist Handbook so amazing?" is not a
     real objection. Prevention: Source objections from actual customer hesitations --
     price ("I can find herb info free online"), format ("I do not read long PDFs"),
     trust ("how is this different from every other herb book"), safety ("can I use
     this if I am on medication"). The hardest questions must be answered first.
  2. Informational answers that do not close. Answering "Is this worth $49?" with a
     feature list restates the pitch without resolving the doubt. Prevention: Every FAQ
     answer must end with a sentence that resolves the objection, not just addresses it.
     The close might be a reframe, a proof point, or a guarantee reference.
  3. Introducing new objections. Answering a question the reader was not asking plants
     a new doubt. Prevention: Limit FAQs to 3-5 questions drawn from real VOC data or
     the offer brief's objection list. Do not invent hypothetical questions.
  4. Defensive or dismissive tone. "Obviously the handbook covers that" is condescending.
     Prevention: Apply the Section 3 register -- "patient clarity, zero condescension."
     Each answer should feel like a thoughtful, complete response from a knowledgeable
     friend.

SELF-EVALUATION CHECKLIST:
  [ ] Are all FAQ questions sourced from real customer objections (not planted softballs)?
  [ ] Is the most common or most serious objection listed first?
  [ ] Does every answer end with a closing statement that resolves the objection (not
      just a restatement of features)?
  [ ] Is the total FAQ count between 3 and 5 questions?
  [ ] Does the tone in every answer match "patient clarity, zero condescension"?
  [ ] Does the FAQ section avoid raising objections the reader is unlikely to have?
```

### CARD 8

```
SECTION TYPE: CTA Section
JOB (one sentence): Convert the reader's accumulated conviction into a single, clear
action with zero ambiguity about what happens next.
SUCCESS STATE: "I know exactly what I am getting, what it costs, and what happens when
I click. I am clicking."
FAILURE STATE: "I am not sure what happens when I click" OR "This feels too pushy" OR
"I scrolled right past it."
MEASURABLE PROXY: CTA click-through rate (target: 3-5% of page visitors on primary CTA;
total across all CTAs: 5-8%).

COMMON FAILURE MODES:
  1. Ambiguous button copy. "Submit" or "Continue" does not tell the reader what she is
     getting. Prevention: Button copy must name the deliverable or the action outcome.
     "Get the Handbook -- $49" passes. "Submit" fails.
  2. CTA overload. More than 3 CTA placements on a single page reads as desperation to
     this audience. Prevention: Maximum 3 CTA instances per page (per Section 2 rules).
     Each must be contextually appropriate to its position.
  3. CTA without adjacent reassurance. A buy button with no guarantee reminder or
     trust signal nearby forces the reader to recall those elements from memory.
     Prevention: Every CTA instance must have at least one reassurance element within
     visual proximity -- guarantee mention, security badge, or "instant delivery"
     microcopy.
  4. Aggressive CTA language. "Buy Now!" or "Claim Your Copy Before It's Gone!" violates
     the brand voice. Prevention: CTA language must pass the Section 3 voice test --
     "warm invitation, quiet confidence." Approved patterns: "Get the Handbook," "Start
     using it today," "Complete My Order."

SELF-EVALUATION CHECKLIST:
  [ ] Does the button copy name the deliverable or the action outcome?
  [ ] Is the price visible on or immediately adjacent to the primary CTA button?
  [ ] Does every CTA instance have a reassurance element (guarantee, security, instant
      delivery) within visual proximity?
  [ ] Is the total number of CTA placements on this page 3 or fewer?
  [ ] Is the CTA language free of urgency words, exclamation points, and pressure tactics?
  [ ] Does the CTA tell the reader what happens next (instant download, email delivery,
      checkout page)?
```

### CARD 9

```
SECTION TYPE: Presell Opening (Editorial Lead)
JOB (one sentence): Hook the reader with a specific, relatable herbal decision-making
frustration in an editorial frame that reads like a wellness article, not an ad.
SUCCESS STATE: "This article is about my exact situation. I want to read the whole thing."
FAILURE STATE: "This is an ad disguised as an article -- I am out" OR "This has nothing
to do with me" OR "I have read this same generic wellness intro a hundred times."
MEASURABLE PROXY: Scroll-to-midpoint rate on the presell page (target: 55%+); time on

COMMON FAILURE MODES:
  1. Breaking editorial frame. Any sales language, product name, price mention, or

     no product mention until the final 25%.
  2. Generic wellness opening. "In today's world of natural health..." is AI-generated

     specific, concrete scenario -- a named herb, a specific decision moment, a

  3. Opening with "I." Starting the presell with the author's story centers the brand,
     not the reader. Prevention: The first sentence must be about the reader's
     experience or a universally relatable scenario. The author enters the narrative
     only after the reader sees herself.
  4. Tone mismatch with the publication context. A presell running on Meta must feel
     like a wellness article the reader chose to click, not interruptive ad copy.
     Prevention: Read the opening aloud and ask: "Would this feel native in a health
     magazine?" If not, adjust register.

SELF-EVALUATION CHECKLIST:
  [ ] Is the product name absent from the first 75% of the presell?
  [ ] Does the opening sentence name a specific herb, scenario, or decision moment
      (not a generic wellness statement)?
  [ ] Does the first sentence center the reader's experience, not the author or brand?
  [ ] Would this opening feel native in a wellness magazine or editorial context?
  [ ] Is the agitation ceiling at Level 3 or below (Level 4 permitted once, later in
      the piece, per Subsection A rules)?
  [ ] Does the opening establish both B1 (herbs can help) and B2 (natural does not
      equal safe) within the first 120 words?
  [ ] If the headline has a PROMISE_CONTRACT, does the lead begin paying off the
      DELIVERY_TEST within the first 120 words?
  [ ] Is the promise delivery integrated with B1/B2 establishment, not bolted on as
      a separate paragraph that disrupts the editorial flow?
```

## SUBSECTION B: CROSS-SECTION FLOW EVALUATION

After drafting a complete page, the agent runs ALL FOUR of the following evaluation checks before the draft is considered review-ready. A failure on any check requires revision before submission.

---

### CHECK 1: BELIEF PROGRESSION CHECK

**Purpose:** Confirm that each section advances the belief chain in the correct sequence, with no skips, no premature introductions, and the Foundation Gate satisfied.

**Evaluation template -- fill in for every section of the drafted page:**

| Section # | Section Name | Primary Belief Advanced | Is the preceding belief already established? (Y/N) | Does this section introduce more than one NEW belief? (Y/N) |
|---|---|---|---|---|

**Yes/No evaluation questions:**

1. Is B1 (herbs can help) established before B3 (ecosystem is broken) appears anywhere on the page?
2. Is B2 (natural does not equal safe) established before B3 appears anywhere on the page?
3. Does B1 and B2 establishment occur in the same section or in adjacent sections (Foundation Gate)?
4. Does B5 (this handbook is different) appear only after B3 (ecosystem broken) and B4 (need a system) are both established?
5. Do B7 (worth $49) and B8 (risk-free) appear together in the same section or in immediately adjacent sections before the primary CTA?
6. Can you name exactly one primary belief for every section? If any section advances two new beliefs, flag it for splitting.
7. Are there any sections that introduce a belief already established earlier? If so, confirm it is REINFORCING (acceptable) rather than RE-INTRODUCING (indicating the first establishment failed).

**Pass criteria:** All 7 questions answered satisfactorily. Any "no" on questions 1-5 is a structural failure requiring section reordering.

---

### CHECK 2: EMOTIONAL ARC CHECK

**Purpose:** Confirm the page follows the expected emotional trajectory and does not trap the reader in a negative register or create tonal whiplash.

**Expected arc shape for the sales page:**

```
Calm recognition (hero)
    ↓
Validated frustration (problem -- never deeper than Level 3)
    ↓
Relief + intellectual curiosity (mechanism)
    ↓
Warm confidence (proof)
    ↓
Practical clarity (offer stack)
    ↓
Steady reassurance (guarantee)
    ↓
Resolved confidence (CTA)
```

**The arc must satisfy a "dip and rise" shape:** emotional register dips once (during agitation), then rises steadily through mechanism, proof, and close. The page must NEVER have a second emotional dip after the proof section.

**Yes/No evaluation questions:**

1. Does the emotional register dip exactly once (in the problem/agitation section) and then trend upward for the remainder of the page?
2. Is the agitation section the lowest emotional point on the page? If any later section is more negative, flag it.
3. Does the mechanism section feel like relief (intellectual curiosity, "there is a solution") rather than continued agitation?
4. Is the proof section warmer and more confident than the mechanism section?
5. Does the guarantee section feel reassuring, not anxious? (A guarantee that re-agitates the problem undoes its own job.)
6. Does the final CTA section feel like a calm, confident invitation -- not a last-ditch pressure play?
7. Are there any register shifts between adjacent sections that lack a bridge sentence per Section 3, Subsection E rules?

**Pass criteria:** All 7 questions answered "yes." Any "no" requires identifying the specific section that breaks the arc and rewriting for register alignment.

---

### CHECK 3: MOMENTUM CHECK

**Purpose:** Confirm that at no point in the page does reading motivation decrease. Every section must give the reader a reason to keep scrolling.

**Red flags -- any of these present means the check fails:**

- Two consecutive sections that agitate without providing value, proof, or mechanism (stacked negativity kills momentum).
- A section that restates the previous section's point without adding new information (the reader feels stalled).
- A section longer than 400 words without a subhead, visual break, or shift in content type (wall-of-text fatigue).
- A proof section placed before the mechanism section (proof for WHAT? -- the reader has no framework to evaluate it).
- A CTA placed before B5 is established (premature ask -- the reader does not yet know what makes this product worth buying).
- An FAQ question that raises a new objection not addressed elsewhere on the page (the reader leaves the FAQ more uncertain than she entered).
- Any section that ends on a closed statement with no forward-pointing element (the reader has no reason to scroll further).

**Yes/No evaluation questions:**

1. Does every section end with an implicit or explicit reason to read the next section?
2. Are there zero instances of two consecutive sections covering the same ground without new information?
3. Is every section under 400 words, or if longer, does it contain at least one subhead or visual break?
4. Does the mechanism section appear before the main proof section?
5. Does the first CTA appear only after B5 (product differentiation) has been established?
6. Does every FAQ answer leave the reader more confident, not less?

**Pass criteria:** All 6 questions answered "yes" and zero red flags present.

---

### CHECK 4: REDUNDANCY CHECK

**Purpose:** Ensure that no specific claim, statistic, or proof point appears more than twice on a single page. Repetition beyond two instances signals either padding or structural disorganization.

**Operating rule:** A specific claim may appear once to ESTABLISH and once to REINFORCE. A third appearance means the page structure is relying on repetition instead of progression.

**Yes/No evaluation questions:**

1. Does any specific statistic (e.g., "300+ herbs") appear more than twice on the page? If yes, cut the weakest instance.
2. Does any specific testimonial quote appear more than once? If yes, remove the duplicate.
3. Is the guarantee stated or paraphrased more than twice? (Once in the guarantee section and once adjacent to the final CTA is the maximum.)
4. Does the price ($49) appear more than three times on the page? (Price is the one exception -- it may appear on the offer stack, each CTA button, and the sticky bar, but not in body copy beyond the offer section.)
5. Does any single benefit claim (e.g., "safety flags for every herb") appear in more than two sections? If yes, the second-weakest instance must be cut or reworded to advance a different angle.
6. Is the problem framing (ecosystem is broken) restated in full after the mechanism section? If yes, the post-mechanism instance must be cut -- the reader has already moved past the problem.

**Pass criteria:** All 6 questions answered "no" (meaning no redundancy violations detected). Any "yes" requires targeted editing to reduce to the two-instance maximum.

### CHECK 5: PROMISE DELIVERY CHECK

**Purpose:** Confirm that the headline's specific promise is delivered in the body copy before the reader's patience expires, and that the delivery is substantial enough to satisfy the curiosity loop opened by the headline.

**Required input:** The headline's PROMISE_CONTRACT (produced by HEADLINE-ENGINE.md Step 4.5). If no Promise Contract exists, this check cannot be run — flag the headline as incomplete.

**Evaluation template — fill in for the drafted page:**

| Field | Contract Says | Body Delivers | Pass? |
|-------|--------------|---------------|-------|
| LOOP_QUESTION | [from contract] | [quote or describe the passage that answers it] | Y/N |
| SPECIFIC_PROMISE | [from contract] | [describe how body satisfies it] | Y/N |
| DELIVERY_TEST | [from contract] | [does the body contain what the test requires?] | Y/N |
| MINIMUM_DELIVERY | [from contract] | [which section actually delivers, and is it before the deadline?] | Y/N |

**Yes/No evaluation questions:**

1. Can you identify at least one specific passage in the body that directly answers the headline's LOOP_QUESTION?
2. Does that passage appear BEFORE the structural pivot to the solution category (advertorial Section 4) or mechanism section (sales page Section 3)?
3. Is the answer substantial (3+ sentences of actual content), not a passing mention or glancing reference?
4. Would a reader who clicked specifically because of the headline feel satisfied that the page delivered what was promised? (This is the critical gut-check — if a reasonable reader would feel bait-and-switched, the check fails regardless of the other answers.)
5. Does the promise delivery integrate with the belief chain (it serves both the headline promise AND a belief from B1-B4), rather than being a bolted-on paragraph that disrupts the page's flow?

**Pass criteria:** All 5 questions answered "yes." Question 4 is the decisive test — if the answer is "no," the page has a trust violation.

**Failure remediation:**
- If questions 1-3 fail: The body needs content added. Return to Sections 1-2 and draft the missing content that satisfies the DELIVERY_TEST.
- If question 4 fails: The headline may be unpayable for this page type. Consider selecting a different headline from the HookBank.
- If question 5 fails: The promise delivery is structurally isolated. Rewrite the delivery passage to simultaneously serve the promise and the belief chain.
