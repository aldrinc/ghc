## 7A: Hook Anatomy

### Three Required Components

Every hook produced under this brand contains exactly three components. If any component is missing, the hook fails the construction check and must be rewritten.

**1. Pattern Interrupt** -- the element that stops the scroll.

This is the first cognitive disruption. It breaks the reader's default state (scrolling, skimming, deleting) by introducing something unexpected enough to pause on. For this brand, the interrupt must operate within the anti-hype register. Approved interrupt mechanisms:

- A specific, counterintuitive claim grounded in evidence ("Most chamomile tea is brewed wrong for sleep")
- A concrete, vivid detail that creates instant imagery ("The herb sitting in your spice rack that interacts with blood thinners")
- A calm contradiction of popular belief ("Elderberry is not for every immune situation")
- A question the reader's mind answers involuntarily ("Do you know which three herbs in your cabinet have real drug interactions?")

Banned interrupt mechanisms: shock language, catastrophizing, ALL CAPS words, emoji-as-interrupt, fabricated statistics, conspiracy framing. These are permanently disqualified per Section 3 anti-patterns and Section 4 compliance rules.

**2. Relevance Signal** -- the element that tells the reader "this is for me."

Without a relevance signal, the pattern interrupt captures attention that immediately dissipates. The relevance signal anchors the interrupt to the reader's identity, situation, or felt problem. Approved relevance mechanisms:

- Identity marker ("If you keep herbs at home but second-guess every dose...")
- Situation specificity ("You have searched three different websites for the same herb and gotten three different answers")
- Problem naming at the reader's awareness level (per Section 5.2 lead strategy rules)

Rule: The relevance signal must match the awareness level of the target audience. An Unaware audience gets identity or emotional-state signals. A Problem-Aware audience gets situation-specific signals. A Product-Aware audience gets product-referencing signals. Mismatch between relevance signal and awareness level is a construction failure.

**3. Implicit Promise** -- the element that tells the reader what continuing gives them.

This is the forward pull. It answers the subconscious question "Why should I keep reading?" without making an explicit claim. Approved promise mechanisms:

- A benefit framed as information ("...and the one safety check that changes how you use it")
- A curiosity gap with payoff potential ("...but there is one thing most herbal guides leave out")
- A mechanism tease ("...until you understand how it actually interacts with your body")
- A resolution signal ("Here is how to know for certain")

Rule: The implicit promise must be payable. If the hook promises information, the copy must deliver that information. If it opens a curiosity gap, the copy must close it. An implicit promise that goes unpaid is a trust violation and is banned under Section 3 anti-pattern #12 (filler that restates without adding).

### The Construction Formula

```
[Pattern Interrupt] + [Relevance Signal] + [Implicit Promise]
```

The three components may appear in any order and may be woven into a single sentence or spread across two to three sentences. The formula is a checklist, not a sentence template. All three must be verifiable in the finished hook.

### Brand-Calibrated Examples

**All three in one sentence:**
"If you use herbs with your family but still Google every dose at midnight, this reference changes that." (Interrupt: "Google every dose at midnight" -- vivid, specific. Relevance: "use herbs with your family." Promise: "this reference changes that.")

**Spread across two sentences:**
"Most herbal guides skip the part where things go wrong. This one starts there." (Interrupt: "skip the part where things go wrong." Relevance: implied -- the reader who worries about safety. Promise: "This one starts there" -- the resource that addresses what others avoid.)

**Question-led:**
"Do you actually know which herbs in your kitchen interact with common medications?" (Interrupt: the question itself, forcing internal response. Relevance: "herbs in your kitchen" -- identity of a home herbalist. Promise: implied -- continue and you will find out.)

## 7B: Hook Archetypes for Herbal Health/Wellness

The following nine archetypes are calibrated to this brand's anti-hype positioning, this audience's trust-first psychology, and the compliance constraints in Section 4. When generating a HookBank, the agent must use at least 4 different archetypes and allocate no more than 30% of hooks to any single archetype.

| # | Archetype | Description | Best Awareness Level | Best Platform | Example Hook (in brand voice) | When NOT to Use |
|---|-----------|-------------|---------------------|---------------|-------------------------------|-----------------|
| 1 | **Problem Callout** | Names the reader's specific frustration with precision. Crystallizes a felt but unarticulated problem. | Problem-Aware | Meta primary text, Presell headline | "You want to use herbs for your family. But every source you find contradicts the last one." | Do not use for Most-Aware (they know the problem; re-stating it stalls the sale). Do not use if the problem statement requires naming a disease or clinical condition (Section 4 violation). |
| 2 | **Identity Callout** | Calls out who the reader is, not what they suffer from. Makes them feel recognized before any claim is made. | Unaware, Problem-Aware | TikTok, Meta video/reel, Email subject line | "You are the person your friends text when their kid has a fever and they want a natural option first." | Do not use for Product-Aware or Most-Aware (identity framing is too indirect; they need offer information). Do not use identity labels banned in Section 3 ("warrior," "goddess," "queen," "mama," "babe"). |
| 3 | **Contrarian Claim** | Challenges a widely held belief within the herbal/wellness community. Creates differentiation by positioning the brand as more honest than the consensus. | Solution-Aware, Problem-Aware | Meta primary text, YouTube organic, Presell headline | "Elderberry is one of the most popular immune herbs. It is also one of the most misunderstood in terms of when not to use it." | Do not use without substantiation. Every contrarian claim must be supportable by referenced research or documented traditional-use caveats. Do not use if the contrarian framing requires conspiracy language or "they don't want you to know" framing (Section 3 ban). |
| 4 | **Curiosity Gap** | Opens an information gap the reader wants closed. Withholds a specific detail that pulls the reader forward. | Problem-Aware, Solution-Aware | Meta primary text, Email subject line, TikTok | "There is one herb in almost every kitchen spice rack that has a real, documented interaction with blood thinners." | Do not use if the gap cannot be closed in the body copy (unpaid curiosity violates Section 3). Do not use for Most-Aware audiences (they want the offer, not a tease). Do not fabricate the gap -- the withheld information must be real and worth knowing. |
| 5 | **Safety Warning** | Leads with a specific, factual safety concern about common herbal use. Unique to this category and this brand's safety-first positioning. | Problem-Aware, Solution-Aware | Meta primary text, YouTube pre-roll, Email subject line | "If you take any prescription medication and use herbal supplements, there is a short list of interactions worth checking before your next dose." | Do not use at agitation levels above 3 (Section 1 agitation scale). Do not name specific diseases or imply diagnosis. Do not catastrophize -- the tone is protective concern, not alarm. Do not use for Unaware audiences (they have no frame for why this matters yet). |
| 6 | **Social Proof Lead** | Opens with a credible, specific proof element -- a reader count, a testimonial snippet, or a verifiable outcome. | Product-Aware, Most-Aware | Meta retargeting, Email, Sales page headline | "Over 12,000 women use this as their go-to reference before reaching for any herb. Here is why." | Do not fabricate or inflate numbers. Do not use testimonials that imply health outcomes without the disclaimers required by Section 4. Do not use for Unaware audiences (proof of a product they do not know exists is meaningless). |
| 7 | **Story/Anecdote** | Opens with a specific, relatable moment or scene. Pulls the reader into a narrative before any claim or product mention. | Unaware, Problem-Aware | TikTok, Meta video/reel, Presell headline, YouTube organic | "Last month a reader emailed us: 'I almost gave my toddler an herb that interacts with his prescription. I had no idea.' That is exactly why this book exists." | Do not use for Most-Aware audiences (they want the deal, not a story). Do not fabricate anecdotes. Do not use stories that invoke medical emergencies or fearmongered health consequences (Section 3 anti-pattern #7). |
| 8 | **Direct Benefit** | Leads with the concrete outcome or utility the reader gains. No buildup, no indirection. | Product-Aware, Most-Aware | Meta retargeting, Email, Sales page headline | "300+ herbs. Safety flags for every one. Dosing references you can actually trust. One book, $49." | Do not use for Unaware or Problem-Aware audiences (they have not yet accepted the solution category; a benefit statement lands flat). Do not overstate benefits -- the product is a reference book, not a health transformation tool (Section 3 anti-pattern #16). |
| 9 | **Expert Insight** | Leverages the author's credentials, research process, or professional experience to frame a claim. Positions the brand as a knowledgeable guide, not a hype merchant. | Solution-Aware, Product-Aware | YouTube organic, Presell headline, Meta primary text, Email | "After reviewing over 300 herbs through the lens of published safety research, here is what we found most guides get wrong." | Do not inflate or fabricate credentials (Section 3, B2 rules). Do not use "leading expert" or "world-renowned" language. Do not frame author as guru -- frame as rigorous researcher who shares what she found. Do not use for Unaware audiences (they do not care about credentials for a product they do not know exists). |

### Archetype Selection Rules

1. Every HookBank of 10+ hooks must contain at least 4 different archetypes.
2. No single archetype may exceed 30% of the total bank.
3. The archetype must match the target awareness level. Consult the "Best Awareness Level" column. Deploying an archetype outside its designated level is a construction failure unless the agent documents a specific strategic rationale.
4. Archetypes 5 (Safety Warning) and 9 (Expert Insight) are high-trust archetypes unique to this brand's positioning. At least one of these two must appear in every HookBank of 10+ hooks.

## 7C: Platform-Specific Hook Adaptation

The same hook concept adapts to each platform's constraints. The agent must modify hooks to fit platform rules before delivery. A hook that passes the anatomy check but violates platform constraints is not deliverable.

| Platform | Character/Time Limit | Hook Constraints | Adaptation Rules | Compliance Notes |
|----------|---------------------|-----------------|------------------|------------------|
| **Meta primary text** | First 125 characters visible before "See more" truncation. Full text up to 2,200 characters. | The complete hook (all 3 components) must land within the first 125 characters. If it cannot, the pattern interrupt and relevance signal must be within 125 characters and the implicit promise must be the first line after the fold. | Front-load the interrupt. No slow buildup. No "In today's world" or throat-clearing openers (Section 3 anti-pattern #1). Write the hook as if only the first 125 characters will be read. | No disease claims in ad copy. No personal-attribute targeting ("Are you suffering from...?"). Health disclaimers required per Section 4 Subsection E. Landing page must match ad claims. |
| **Meta video/reel** | First 1-3 seconds determine retention. Text overlay + spoken word + visual must all hook simultaneously. | Text overlay carries the pattern interrupt. Spoken word delivers the relevance signal. Visual establishes context or emotional tone. All three must land within 3 seconds. | Write the text overlay as a standalone interrupt (8-12 words maximum). The spoken-word script opens mid-thought, not with greetings or preamble. Visual must not require explanation. | Same claim restrictions as primary text. Branded content toggle required for partnerships. On-screen disclaimers for health-related claims. |
| **TikTok** | 1-2 seconds to hook before the swipe. Native, authentic tone required. 150-character text overlay limit for readability. | Open mid-thought. No introductions, no "Hey guys," no brand name in the first 2 seconds. The first spoken sentence is the pattern interrupt. Text overlay reinforces or adds the curiosity gap. | Write as if the viewer stumbled into the middle of a conversation. Use contrarian or curiosity archetypes. Specificity outperforms vague claims. Tone must pass the "real person, not advertisement" test. | No health condition claims. No claims discouraging professional consultation. AI monitoring is active -- avoid even near-violations. Branded content toggle required for paid partnerships. |
| **YouTube pre-roll** | 5-second skip window. Hook must hold through the skip decision. | The most compelling claim or most specific detail must appear in the first sentence (under 5 seconds of spoken word). Do not open with questions that are easy to dismiss. Do not open with "What if I told you..." | First sentence = the single most interesting, specific thing about the content. Second sentence = relevance signal that earns 5 more seconds. Promise must land by second 10. Front-load value; earn continued attention in increments. | No false or exaggerated health claims in ad or on landing page. No focus on specific body parts or health conditions. Landing page is reviewed alongside ad content. Google enforces account-level consequences without warning. |
| **YouTube organic** | First 15-30 seconds set retention. Longer hook runway than pre-roll. | Pattern interrupt in the first sentence. Relevance signal by second sentence. Promise can develop through second 15. More room for story or expert-insight archetypes. | Match the hook to the search intent that brought the viewer. If the video targets "how to use herbs safely," the hook must reference safety within 10 seconds. Specificity and credentials outperform hype. | Same compliance as pre-roll. Landing page and video content both evaluated. Testimonials in video require disclaimers. |
| **Email subject line + preview text** | Subject line: 40-60 characters for display. Preview text: approximately 90 additional characters. | Subject line carries the pattern interrupt. Preview text carries the relevance signal or implicit promise. They function as a pair, not independently. | Write subject line in lowercase-friendly, conversational tone per Section 3 A1 (4-9 words). No ALL CAPS words. Preview text must extend the subject line's thought, not repeat it. The pair must create enough pull to earn the open without overpromising. | Subject line must accurately reflect email content (CAN-SPAM). No deceptive subject lines. No disease claims. Health disclaimers required in email body if health topics are referenced. |
| **Presell headline** | No character limit. Reader arrives from an ad -- message match is mandatory. | The headline must feel like the natural continuation of the ad hook that sent the reader here. If the ad promised a specific insight, the headline must reference that insight. Headline mismatch causes immediate bounce. | Write the presell headline after the ad hook, not before. The presell headline answers or extends the ad's implicit promise. Use editorial, not sales, framing. The headline should read like a magazine feature, not an advertisement. | All Section 4 claim restrictions apply. Landing page is evaluated alongside ad content by all platforms. Claims on the presell cannot exceed claims in the ad. |
| **Sales page headline** | No character limit. First element the reader sees. | Must confirm relevance and create pull to scroll. If the reader arrives from a presell, reference what they just learned. If from an ad, match the ad promise. If from direct traffic, lead with the product's core differentiator. | Specificity outperforms cleverness. State the product's core value proposition in concrete terms. Use the Section 5.2 headline formula for the asset's working awareness level. One clear idea per headline -- do not stack multiple claims. | All Section 4 restrictions. No banned phrases from Section 4 Subsection C. The headline sets the claim ceiling for the entire page -- nothing below it can exceed what the headline promises. |

### Adaptation Process

The agent follows this sequence when adapting a hook concept across platforms:

1. Write the hook at full length with all three components clearly articulated.
2. Identify the target platform from the brief.
3. Apply the character/time constraint. If the full hook does not fit, prioritize: pattern interrupt first, relevance signal second, implicit promise third.
4. Check the compliance notes for the target platform against Section 4.
5. Verify the adapted hook still passes the three-component anatomy check. If a component was cut for space, it must appear immediately after the fold, in the preview text, or in the first seconds of body copy.

## 7D: Meaningful Differentiation Standard

### The Four Dimensions of Differentiation

Two hooks are meaningfully different when they diverge on at least 2 of these 4 dimensions:

**Dimension 1: Archetype.** The hooks use different archetypes from the Section 7B table. A Problem Callout and a Contrarian Claim are different archetypes even if they address the same topic.

**Dimension 2: Entry Angle.** The hooks enter through different psychological frames:
- Problem entry: leads with what is wrong ("You cannot find consistent dosing information")
- Solution entry: leads with what exists to fix it ("A reference that flags every known interaction")
- Identity entry: leads with who the reader is ("You are careful about what your family puts in their bodies")

Two hooks using the same archetype but different entry angles are meaningfully different on this dimension.

**Dimension 3: Belief Targeted.** The hooks address different beliefs from the B1-B8 chain defined in Section 1. A hook targeting B2 ("natural does not mean safe") and a hook targeting B3 ("the info ecosystem is broken") are meaningfully different on this dimension. Reference the belief dependency map in Section 1 Subsection A, 2.2.

**Dimension 4: Emotional Register.** The hooks operate in different registers from the Section 3 Subsection D emotional register map:
- Clinical: data-forward, evidence-referencing, measured
- Empathetic: warm, validating, protective
- Provocative: challenging, contrarian, direct (not aggressive -- provocative within brand voice boundaries)

### What Does NOT Count as Meaningfully Different

The following modifications fail the differentiation check. They are not meaningfully different and must not be counted as separate hooks in a HookBank:

- **Synonym swaps.** Replacing "confused" with "overwhelmed" or "frustrated" without changing archetype, angle, belief, or register.
- **Word reordering.** Moving the same components into a different sentence order without changing meaning or psychological entry point.
- **Additions or deletions that preserve the core claim.** Adding a modifier ("really confused" vs. "confused") or removing a word does not create a new hook.
- **Tonal micro-shifts within the same register.** Slightly warmer or slightly cooler versions of the same empathetic hook are not different hooks.
- **Platform-only reformats.** A Meta-adapted version and a TikTok-adapted version of the same hook concept count as one hook, not two. Platform adaptation is execution, not differentiation.

### Minimum Differentiation Standard for HookBank Output

| Requirement | Minimum Threshold |
|-------------|-------------------|
| Pairwise differentiation | Every hook must differ from every other hook on at least 2 of 4 dimensions. |
| Archetype coverage | The full bank must use at least 4 different archetypes. |
| Belief coverage | The full bank must target at least 3 different beliefs from the B1-B8 chain. |
| Register coverage | The full bank must use at least 3 different emotional registers. |
| Brand-specific archetype inclusion | At least 1 hook must use archetype 5 (Safety Warning) or archetype 9 (Expert Insight) per bank of 10+ hooks. |

### HookBank Tagging Requirement

Every hook delivered in a HookBank must be tagged with four metadata fields:

```
HOOK: [The hook text]
ARCHETYPE: [Number and name from 7B table]
BELIEF TARGETED: [B1-B8 identifier]
EMOTIONAL REGISTER: [Clinical / Empathetic / Provocative]
TARGET AWARENESS LEVEL: [Unaware / Problem-Aware / Solution-Aware / Product-Aware / Most-Aware]
```

The agent runs the differentiation check after generating the full bank. If the bank fails any threshold in the table above, the agent must replace hooks until all thresholds are met. The differentiation check is a delivery gate -- no HookBank ships without passing it.
