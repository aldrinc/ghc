# Subsection B: Sentence-Level Craft Rules
## Operating Rules for Rhythm, Specificity, Bullets, Transitions & Retention

**Brand context:** The Honest Herbalist Handbook | $49 digital herbal reference | DTC health/wellness
**Audience:** Women 25-55, safety-conscious, anti-hype, "crunchy but not anti-science," Stage 5 market sophistication
**Brand voice anchor:** No hype. No woo. No fearmongering. Honest, safety-first, natural-first but not reckless.

---

## 1. READABILITY STANDARD (Non-Negotiable)

**Target reading level: 5th-7th grade Flesch-Kincaid.**

This is the single most data-backed conversion driver in health/wellness DTC copy. Unbounce's 2024 dataset (464M visitors, health/wellness specific) shows 10.8% median conversion at this level -- dropping 48% at 8th-9th grade and 56% at 10th-12th grade. The trend is accelerating: difficult words hurt conversion 62% more in 2024 than in 2020.

| Measure | Target |
|---|---|
| Flesch Reading Ease | 65-75 |
| Flesch-Kincaid Grade | 5-7 |
| Gunning Fog Index | 7-9 |
| Average sentence length | 12-16 words |
| Max sentence length | 25 words (hard ceiling) |

**Rule:** If a mechanism section or scientific explanation pushes above 7th-grade level, rewrite it. The mechanism should feel like an "aha" moment, not a lecture. If a 14-year-old can't follow it, the copy fails. Complex ideas require simpler words, not simpler ideas.

**Word choice hierarchy:**
- Use short, common words by default (1-2 syllables)
- Use precise scientific terms only when they ADD credibility (e.g., "cortisol" is acceptable because the audience knows it; "hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal axis" is not -- say "your stress response system")
- Replace any word the audience would need to look up

## 2. SPECIFICITY RULES

**Core rule: Specific > vague. Always. No exceptions in this category.**

In a Stage 5 market (health/wellness herbal), generic claims are invisible. The audience has heard every promise. Specificity is the only mechanism that creates the feeling of "this is different from everything I've tried."

### 2.1 The Specificity Hierarchy (use the highest level available)

| Level | Example | When to Use |
|---|---|---|
| **Exact number + source** | "A 2023 NCCIH-funded trial with 142 participants found valerian reduced sleep onset by 14 minutes" | When verifiable data exists. Always preferred. |
| **Specific-but-unrounded number** | "73% of users reported falling asleep faster within the first week" | When survey/user data exists. Non-rounded numbers feel measured, not invented. |
| **Named mechanism** | "Chamomile binds to GABA receptors -- the same receptors targeted by prescription sleep aids -- but without the dependency risk" | When explaining how something works. Name the specific pathway. |
| **Sensory-concrete language** | "That 2am wakeup where your mind starts replaying every conversation from the day" | When quantification is impossible. Name the exact sensation, the exact moment, the exact feeling. |
| **Category-specific detail** | "The difference between German chamomile and Roman chamomile -- and why only one belongs in your sleep tincture" | When differentiating within the herbal space. Specifics the audience recognizes as insider knowledge. |

### 2.2 The "Only This Product" Test

After writing any claim, benefit, or bullet, apply this test: **Could this sentence appear on a competitor's page without modification?**

- If yes: it's too generic. Rewrite until it could only describe The Honest Herbalist Handbook.
- "Helps you sleep better" -- fails (could be any product)
- "Shows you exactly which 3 herbs are evidence-backed for sleep, which ones interact with SSRIs, and which 'natural sleep aids' are actually stimulants in disguise" -- passes (specific to this product's promise)

### 2.3 Specificity Under Compliance Constraints

Health/wellness copy must be specific AND compliant. Use these reframe patterns:

| What You Want to Say | Compliant + Specific Version | Why It Works |
|---|---|---|
| "This herb cures your anxiety" | "Ashwagandha has been studied in 5 randomized controlled trials for stress. In one (300mg twice daily, 60 days), participants reported a 44% greater reduction in stress scores vs. placebo." | Cites specific study, specific dose, specific result. No disease claim. |
| "Fixes your sleep" | "The handbook's sleep chapter covers 6 herbs with human clinical evidence for sleep onset and sleep quality -- including which ones are safe with blood pressure medications" | Specific to product structure, references safety (trust signal), no disease claim. |
| "Reduces inflammation" | "Three herbs in the joint comfort section have been used in Ayurvedic practice for 3,000+ years. Modern RCTs have since measured their effects on inflammatory markers." | Bridges tradition + modern evidence. References data without overclaiming. |

**The operating principle:** Be specific about WHAT the evidence shows, HOW the product is organized, and WHAT the reader will be able to do -- never about disease treatment or guaranteed outcomes.

## 3. SENTENCE RHYTHM AND CADENCE

### 3.1 The Cadence Rule

Vary sentence length deliberately and unpredictably. Monotonous rhythm causes reader fatigue regardless of content quality.

**The three sentence types:**

| Type | Length | Purpose | Usage |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Punch** | 3-7 words | Emphasis, emotional landing, pace break | After a longer setup. After proof. Before a new idea. Never more than 2 in a row. |
| **Workhorse** | 8-16 words | Claims, transitions, explanations | The default. Most sentences should be in this range. |
| **Builder** | 17-25 words | Storytelling momentum, complex-but-clear ideas | Sparingly. Max 1 per paragraph. Never back-to-back. Always followed by a punch or workhorse. |

**Cadence example (what good rhythm looks like for this brand):**

> Your medicine cabinet probably has melatonin in it. [Workhorse - 8 words]
> Most people's does. [Punch - 3 words]
> But here's what most herbal guides won't tell you: melatonin isn't an herb, it's a hormone -- and long-term use can suppress your body's own production. [Builder - 25 words]
> That's not a scare tactic. [Punch - 5 words]
> It's on page 12 of the handbook, with the studies cited. [Workhorse - 11 words]

### 3.2 Paragraph Rules

| Context | Max Sentences Per Paragraph | Reasoning |
|---|---|---|
| Sales page | 1-3 | Mobile-first reading. Dense paragraphs don't get read. |
| Presell advertorial | 2-4 | Slightly longer to maintain editorial frame. |
| Email | 1-2 | Inbox scanning behavior. One idea per visual block. |
| Mechanism section | 2-3 | Complex ideas need whitespace between them. |

**Hard rule:** No paragraph exceeds 4 sentences. If it does, split it. No exceptions.

### 3.3 The End-of-Sentence Rule

The most important word or phrase goes at the END of the sentence, not buried in the middle.

- Weak: "You can find detailed safety information for every herb in this handbook." (Emphasis lands on "handbook" -- the least important word)
- Strong: "Every herb in this handbook includes detailed safety information." (Emphasis lands on "safety information" -- the core value proposition)
- Strongest: "Every herb. Every dose. Every interaction risk. It's all in the safety notes." (Punch cadence puts emphasis on "safety notes")

### 3.4 One Idea Per Sentence

If a sentence contains two distinct ideas, split it. This is the #1 readability fix.

- Before: "The handbook covers 47 herbs with safety profiles and it also includes a symptom-first index so you can look things up by what's bothering you."
- After: "The handbook covers 47 herbs. Each one includes a full safety profile. And there's a symptom-first index -- so you start with what's bothering you, not what you hope might help."

## 4. BULLET MECHANICS

### 4.1 The Bullet Formula for This Brand

Traditional DR fascination bullets (hype-heavy curiosity) do NOT match this brand's positioning. The audience is anti-hype, Stage 5 sophisticated, and trust-sensitive.

**The Honest Herbalist bullet formula:**

```
[Specific fact or mechanism] + [Practical benefit the reader can use] + [Trust signal or safety anchor]
```

This formula replaces the standard `[curiosity hook] + [implied benefit] + [withhold]` pattern used in hype-driven DR copy. The difference: we build trust through transparency and specificity rather than through information withholding.

### 4.2 Bullet Styles (Ranked by Brand Fit)

**Style 1: Proof-Anchored Bullet (Primary -- use for 40-50% of bullets)**
Combines a specific claim with an embedded credibility marker.

- "The 3 herbs with the strongest clinical evidence for sleep -- including one studied in 12 randomized controlled trials (page 34)"
- "Why German chamomile and Roman chamomile aren't interchangeable -- and which one interacts with blood thinners (page 61, Safety Flags section)"

The page/chapter reference ("finders") makes each bullet feel real and verifiable. It signals: this isn't vapor -- it's in the book.

**Style 2: Contrarian Twist Bullet (Secondary -- use for 20-30% of bullets)**
Challenges what the reader assumes they know. Aligns with their existing distrust of mainstream advice.

- "The popular 'immune booster' that actually suppresses immune function if you take it for more than 2 weeks straight"
- "Why the #1 bestselling 'herbal remedies' book on Amazon was flagged as 82% likely AI-generated -- and what to check before trusting any herbal resource"

**Rule:** Every contrarian bullet must be backed by evidence in the product. Never contradict without proof. If the reader opens the book to verify and can't, you've lost them permanently.

**Style 3: Safety-Warning Bullet (Use for 15-20% of bullets)**
Leverages the audience's core fear (accidentally hurting themselves or their family) and positions the handbook as the safety net.

- "5 common herbs that interact dangerously with SSRIs, birth control, and blood thinners -- cross-referenced in the Interaction Awareness section"
- "The herb every 'natural parenting' blog recommends for kids that most herbalists actually advise against before age 6"

**Style 4: Pleasant Surprise Bullet (Use for 10-15% of bullets)**
Delivers unexpected, positive, layered information. Builds trust by giving away value.

- "One kitchen-cabinet herb that doubles as a first-aid antiseptic, a digestive aid, AND a mild sedative -- with prep instructions for all three uses"
- "The $3 herb that outperformed a $40/month supplement in a head-to-head university trial for stress (and you can grow it on a windowsill)"

### 4.3 Bullet Anti-Patterns (Never Use)

| Anti-Pattern | Why It Fails for This Audience |
|---|---|
| "The SHOCKING secret doctors don't want you to know..." | Conspiracy framing. Audience is "natural-first but not anti-science." This alienates them. |
| "The weird trick that..." | Clickbait register. Signals low-quality content -- exactly what they're trying to avoid. |
| Vague withholding: "A special herb that changes everything" | No specificity. Feels like every bad Amazon herb book they've been burned by. |
| Multiple exclamation points or ALL CAPS in bullets | Hype signals. Instant credibility loss. |
| Bullets that could describe any herb book | Fails the "Only This Product" test. Delete and rewrite. |

### 4.4 Bullet Quantity and Placement Rules

| Context | Bullet Count | Placement Logic |
|---|---|---|
| Offer stack (what's inside the handbook) | 7-10 bullets, front-load strongest 3 | After the offer is introduced, before the price. Each bullet represents a chapter or major feature. |
| Bonus descriptions | 3-5 per bonus | Below each bonus name. Each bullet = a specific thing they'll get. |
| "What you'll learn" section | 10-15, organized by topic cluster | Mid-page. Group by theme (sleep, stress, digestion, kids/family, first aid). |
| Email body | 3-5 maximum | After the hook, before the CTA. Each bullet = one reason to click. |

**Diversity rule:** In any set of 10+ bullets, use at least 3 of the 4 styles above. No more than 3 consecutive bullets of the same style.

## 5. TRANSITION CRAFT

### 5.1 The Forward Momentum Rule

Every section ending must create a pull into the next section. The reader should feel like stopping mid-page is harder than continuing. This is achieved through three techniques, layered:

### 5.2 Transition Techniques (Ranked by Brand Fit)

**Technique 1: The Logical Bridge (Primary -- use at most section transitions)**
The end of one section naturally raises a question that the next section answers.

- End of problem section: "So if the real problem isn't herbs themselves -- but the broken information ecosystem around them -- what would a trustworthy guide actually look like?"
- End of mechanism section: "That's how the handbook is structured. But does it actually work in practice?"

**Rule:** The bridge should feel like the reader's own next thought. If it feels forced, the section order may be wrong.

**Technique 2: Bucket Brigades (Use at natural stopping points within sections)**
Short transitional phrases on their own line. These exploit the Zeigarnik Effect -- open loops the brain needs to close.

Approved for this brand:
- "Here's the thing."
- "But there's a catch."
- "And that's not even the most important part."
- "Let me explain."
- "Which brings us to the real question."

**Not approved** (too hype-y for this brand):
- "But WAIT -- there's more!"
- "You won't BELIEVE what happened next..."
- "Here's where it gets REALLY interesting..."

**Placement:** Every 300-500 words in long-form copy. No more frequently -- overuse makes them invisible.

**Technique 3: The Open Loop (Use sparingly -- max 2 per page)**
Tease information that won't be resolved for several sections. Creates sustained forward pull.

- "In a moment, I'll show you the one section of the handbook that parents tell us they keep photocopied on their fridge. But first..."
- "There's a reason we included a 'Confidence Level' rating for every single remedy. I'll explain that system in a minute -- but it solves a problem you've probably already noticed."

**Rule:** Every open loop must be closed. If you tease something, deliver on it. An unfulfilled promise erodes the trust this brand is built on.

### 5.3 Transition Anti-Patterns

| Anti-Pattern | Why It Fails |
|---|---|
| "But wait, there's more!" | Template copy signal. Audience has seen it 10,000 times. |
| "Here's where it gets interesting..." | Implies everything before was boring. Also formulaic. |
| No transition at all (section just ends) | Creates a natural stopping point. Reader drops off. |
| Transition that repeats the previous section | Kills momentum. Reader thinks "I already read this." |
| Over-promising the next section ("The next part will blow your mind") | Hype register. If the next section is good, it doesn't need a breathless intro. |

## 6. PATTERN INTERRUPTS AND RETENTION DEVICES

### 6.1 Approved Retention Devices

| Device | When to Use | Frequency |
|---|---|---|
| **Crossheads (sub-headlines)** | Every 3-4 paragraphs minimum | Mandatory throughout all long-form copy. Each crosshead should be standalone-compelling -- a scanner should get the page's argument just from crossheads. |
| **Belief validation opener** | Start of new sections, especially after proof | When the reader already suspects something is true, say it back to them. "You've probably noticed that every herbal blog contradicts the last one you read." Creates instant trust. |
| **Specific data as interrupts** | Between narrative sections | A single statistic on its own line or in a callout box breaks visual monotony AND adds credibility. "82% of herbal remedy books on Amazon in 2025 were likely AI-generated." |
| **Format shifts** | Every 400-600 words | Bold, italics, boxed callouts, pull quotes, comparison tables, mini-lists. Prevent wall-of-text fatigue. |
| **Question-then-answer** | Once per major section | A direct question ("So how do you know which herbs are actually safe during pregnancy?") followed by the answer. Mirrors the reader's internal dialogue. |

### 6.2 Devices to Avoid

| Device | Why It's Wrong for This Brand |
|---|---|
| Countdown timers (unless real enrollment cap) | Fake scarcity. Audience detects it instantly. |
| "Scientists HATE this" framing | Conspiracy adjacent. Brand is "evidence-aware, not anti-science." |
| Excessive ellipses for false suspense ("And then... I discovered...") | Manipulative register. Signals low-quality content. |
| Exclamation-heavy emphasis ("This changes EVERYTHING!") | Hype register. Every exclamation mark costs credibility with this audience. |
| Multiple open loops without resolution | Feels like bait. Audience is explicitly tired of being strung along by clickbait health content. |

### 6.3 The Payoff Rule

**Every retention device must be payable.** If you open a loop, close it. If you create a curiosity gap, deliver on it. If you tease a statistic, cite the source. The Honest Herbalist brand is built on the promise of being the antidote to information manipulation. Copy that withholds, teases, or deceives -- even mildly -- contradicts the core brand promise and accelerates audience loss.

## SELF-EVALUATION CHECKLIST

After writing any copy block, the agent runs this checklist:

```
CRAFT CHECK — [Section Name] — [Date]

READABILITY
[ ] Flesch-Kincaid grade level is between 5-7
[ ] No sentence exceeds 25 words
[ ] No paragraph exceeds 4 sentences
[ ] No word requires specialized knowledge to understand (unless it's a term the audience already uses, like "cortisol" or "tincture")

SPECIFICITY
[ ] Every claim includes at least one specific detail (number, name, mechanism, sensory language)
[ ] No sentence could appear unchanged on a competitor's page (the "Only This Product" test)
[ ] Compliant claims use the reframe patterns (specific about evidence/structure/capability, not about disease treatment)

RHYTHM
[ ] Sentence lengths vary across all three types (punch, workhorse, builder)
[ ] No more than 2 punch sentences in a row
[ ] No more than 1 builder sentence per paragraph
[ ] The most important word/phrase in each sentence is at the end

BULLETS
[ ] Every bullet uses one of the 4 approved styles
[ ] No more than 3 consecutive bullets of the same style
[ ] Every bullet passes the "Only This Product" test
[ ] No hype language, ALL CAPS, or multiple exclamation marks

TRANSITIONS
[ ] Every section ending creates forward pull (logical bridge, bucket brigade, or open loop)
[ ] No formulaic transitions ("But wait," "Here's where it gets interesting")
[ ] All open loops are resolved within the page
[ ] Bucket brigades are spaced 300-500 words apart (not more frequent)

RETENTION
[ ] Crossheads appear every 3-4 paragraphs
[ ] At least one format shift (bold, callout, table, list) per 400-600 words
[ ] No forbidden retention devices (countdown timers, conspiracy framing, hype emphasis)
[ ] Every retention device delivers on its promise
```

## EVIDENCE BASE

These rules are derived from:

- **Unbounce 2024 Conversion Benchmark Report**: 464M visitors, 57M conversions, 41K+ landing pages. Health/wellness specific readability data.
- **Portent Readability Study**: R-squared 0.114 (readability explains ~11% of conversion variance; ~13% for ecommerce).
- **PMC Academic Study**: Machine learning prediction of conversion rates from text readability. Optimal Gunning Fog ~8.
- **Mike Pavlish**: $418M in supplement sales. "The more hyperbole and power words you put into a headline, the worse your response." Non-hype headline generated 237% more orders than hype version.
- **Stefan Georgi (RMBC Method)**: Fascination bullet placement and formula. Specific benefit + instant gratification + curiosity.
- **Todd Brown (Unique Mechanism)**: In sophisticated markets, specificity through mechanism differentiation outperforms bigger claims.
- **Gary Bencivenga**: Proof-anchored fascination bullets. Page number "finders." "Never make your claim bigger than your proof."
- **Copy Hackers / Joanna Wiebe**: Front-loading, kernel sentences, the 5-step fascination process.
- **Conversion Rate Experts**: Crossheads, bucket brigades, open loops as anti-drop-off system. CRE long-page tests showing 25-78% conversion lifts.
- **AG1, Ritual, Seed brand analysis**: Honest-brand copywriting patterns that convert in trust-sensitive health markets.
- **Eugene Schwartz (Market Sophistication)**: Stage 5 market dynamics -- identification and community over promises and mechanisms.
- **Originality.AI Study**: 82% of Amazon herbal books likely AI-generated in 2025 -- confirming the audience's trust crisis.
