## FDA Claim Hierarchy

Even though this is a book and not a supplement, the FTC evaluates the "net impression" of advertising on the consumer. If an ad for an herbal reference book implies readers will achieve health outcomes, the FTC treats those implied claims the same as direct product claims.

### Structure/Function Claims -- USABLE WITH CARE
These describe how a nutrient or ingredient affects the normal structure or function of the body.

- Acceptable framing: "Learn how herbalists have traditionally used chamomile to support relaxation"
- Acceptable framing: "Discover which herbs are studied for their role in supporting immune function"
- The book itself does not need to carry the FDA disclaimer, but ad copy should not make claims that would require such a disclaimer if the product were a supplement.

### Health Claims -- HANDLE WITH EXTREME CAUTION
These describe a relationship between a substance and reduced risk of disease. FDA must authorize these claims for supplement labeling.

- Problematic: "This handbook shows you how to reduce your risk of heart disease with herbs"
- Safer: "Explore the research behind herbs studied in the context of cardiovascular wellness"

### Disease Claims -- PROHIBITED IN ALL MARKETING COPY
Any claim that a product (or, by strong implication, the information in a product) can diagnose, cure, mitigate, treat, or prevent a disease is a drug claim under FDA rules and a deceptive claim under FTC rules if unsubstantiated.

- Prohibited even in educational framing: "Learn how to treat your arthritis naturally"
- Prohibited: "The herbal cures for Type 2 diabetes your doctor won't tell you about"

## FTC Guidelines for Marketing Educational Health Products

Key principles from the FTC's Health Products Compliance Guidance:

- **Net Impression Standard:** The FTC evaluates the overall impression an ad conveys to a reasonable consumer, not just individual words. If the totality of an ad implies the consumer will achieve health outcomes by reading the book, that implication is a health claim requiring substantiation.
- **Third-Party Literature Rule:** The FTC does not regulate statements in independently written books, BUT advertisers cannot deceptively use those statements in ads. Quoting a passage from the handbook in an ad that implies a health outcome makes the advertiser liable for that implied claim.
- **Substantiation Before Dissemination:** All health benefit claims in advertising must be substantiated BEFORE the ad runs. "Competent and reliable scientific evidence" generally means randomized controlled human clinical trials. Animal studies and in vitro research alone are insufficient.

## Testimonial Rules

Under the FTC's Endorsement Guides (revised 2023, current as of 2026):

- **"Results not typical" disclaimers are insufficient.** The FTC tested these and found they do not reduce consumer perception that results are representative. If a testimonial reports dramatic results, the ad must disclose what results a typical consumer can actually expect.
- **Two-option rule:** Either (a) have proof the testimonial results are typical, or (b) clearly and conspicuously disclose generally expected performance.
- **Scientific substantiation required:** Testimonials cannot be used to make health claims the advertiser could not substantiate directly. A reader saying "This book helped me manage my blood pressure" in an ad implies the book's content achieves that outcome -- requiring the same substantiation as a direct claim.
- **Material connections must be disclosed:** Any relationship between endorser and seller (free copy, affiliate commission, payment) must be clearly disclosed.

## Disclaimer Templates

### A. Educational Health Content Marketing Disclaimer
Use on all advertising assets (ads, landing pages, emails):

> "The Honest Herbalist Handbook is an educational reference. It is not intended to provide medical advice, diagnosis, or treatment. The information in this handbook is not a substitute for professional medical advice. Always consult a qualified healthcare provider before making changes to your health regimen."

### B. Testimonial Disclaimer
Must accompany every testimonial that references a health-related experience:

> "Individual experiences shared are personal and not representative of typical results. This handbook is an educational resource; individual outcomes from applying any information will vary. Consult a healthcare professional regarding your specific health needs."

Per FTC guidelines, if a testimonial implies results that exceed what is generally expected, you must also disclose what results are typical. "Results not typical" alone is NOT sufficient.

### C. Affiliate/Sponsorship Disclosure
Required whenever a paid or material relationship exists:

> "This post contains affiliate links. If you purchase through these links, [Name/Publication] may receive a commission at no additional cost to you."

**Platform-specific placement:**
- Meta/Instagram: In the first line of caption text; use platform's Paid Partnership tag
- TikTok: In video itself (spoken or on-screen text in first 3 seconds); use branded content toggle
- YouTube: Use paid promotion checkbox; verbal disclosure in first 30 seconds
- Email: In the header area, clearly labeled

### D. Email Marketing Disclaimers
Every marketing email must include:
- Clear identification that this is an advertisement or marketing communication
- Sender's valid physical postal address
- Functional unsubscribe link with clear instructions

For UK recipients: additional PECR/GDPR requirements apply (recipients must have opted in).

## Pre-Submission Compliance Checklist

### Claim Type Verification
- [ ] No disease claims present (diagnose, cure, mitigate, treat, prevent any named disease)
- [ ] No implied disease claims (referencing symptoms, named conditions, or specific biomarkers)
- [ ] Structure/function language framed as educational content the book covers -- not outcomes the reader will achieve
- [ ] No claims that reading the book will produce specific health outcomes
- [ ] No conspiracy or suppression framing

### Banned Phrase Scan
- [ ] Copy scanned against all 30 banned phrases
- [ ] No specific numeric health claims
- [ ] No before/after transformation language
- [ ] No personal-attribute targeting language

### Disclaimer Presence and Placement
- [ ] Educational disclaimer present and properly placed
- [ ] Testimonial disclaimers adjacent to every testimonial
- [ ] Testimonials disclose generally expected results
- [ ] Affiliate/sponsorship disclosures present before first commercial link
- [ ] Email: physical address, unsubscribe link, and ad identification all present

### Testimonial Compliance
- [ ] Each testimonial reflects genuine, current reader experience
- [ ] No testimonial implies health outcomes exceeding typical experience
- [ ] Material connections disclosed
- [ ] No testimonials from health professionals implying medical endorsement

### Urgency and Scarcity Claim Accuracy
- [ ] Any countdown timer reflects a real deadline
- [ ] "Limited time" claims are truthful and time-bound
- [ ] No artificial scarcity on digital goods
- [ ] No false urgency based on health fears

### UK-Specific Checks (If Targeting UK)
- [ ] No health claims absent from the GB Nutrition and Health Claims Register
- [ ] No medicinal claims for unregistered herbal products
- [ ] "Based on traditional use" qualifier present where referencing traditional herbal uses
- [ ] No individual health professional endorsements in ads
