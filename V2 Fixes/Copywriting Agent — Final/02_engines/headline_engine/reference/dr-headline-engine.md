## The 7 Laws -- Quick Reference Cards

### Law 1: Open Loop Engineering
- **Core:** Create cognitive tension via incomplete ideas, contradictions, missing mechanisms.
- **Test:** Does the headline leave the reader needing to know more?
- **Brand guardrail:** Every loop must close in the body copy. No conspiracy framing. No fabricated gaps.
- **Quick example:** "There is one herb in almost every kitchen spice rack that has a real, documented interaction with blood thinners."

### Law 2: Pain Naming
- **Core:** Name the wound before offering the bandage. Call out the struggle, name the enemy, validate the conflict.
- **Test:** Would the reader say "that is exactly my problem" after reading the headline?
- **Brand guardrail:** Agitation level 3 max. No disease naming. No catastrophizing. Protective concern, not alarm.
- **Quick example:** "You want to use herbs for your family. But every source you find contradicts the last one."

### Law 3: Unique Mechanism Promise
- **Core:** Promise a result they have never heard said this way before. Add a mechanism to make it novel and credible.
- **Test:** Does the headline offer an explanation the reader has not encountered?
- **Brand guardrail:** No health-outcome promises. No "miracle/breakthrough/revolutionary." Structure/function claims only.
- **Quick example:** "Why most herb-drug interaction charts are missing the 3 factors that actually determine your risk."

### Law 4: Specificity Creates Trust
- **Core:** Concrete numbers, unusual numbers, descriptors, timeframes. Specificity signals insider knowledge.
- **Test:** Can you point to at least one concrete, verifiable detail in the headline?
- **Brand guardrail:** No fabricated numbers. No inflated stats. Exact numbers over round numbers when available.
- **Quick example:** "300+ herbs. Safety flags for every one. Dosing references you can actually trust."

### Law 5: Simplicity Is Persuasion
- **Core:** Short words, short phrases, linear structure, no multi-clause sentences. Understood in a single pass.
- **Test:** Can someone grasp the headline's meaning in under 3 seconds?
- **Brand guardrail:** No jargon, no semicolons connecting independent clauses, no nested subordinate clauses.
- **Quick example:** "This one starts there."

### Law 6: Credibility Signals
- **Core:** Reference sources, real events, measurable outcomes, personal experiences.
- **Test:** Does the headline contain at least one element that grounds its claim in reality?
- **Brand guardrail:** No inflated credentials. No guru positioning. Author framed as rigorous researcher.
- **Quick example:** "After reviewing over 300 herbs through the lens of published safety research, here is what we found most guides get wrong."

### Law 7: Time Compression
- **Core:** Shorter timeframes create stronger desire. Near-term outcomes override hesitation.
- **Test:** Does the headline contain a timeframe that makes the benefit feel immediately accessible?
- **Brand guardrail:** Time compression applies to information access and safety checks, not health outcomes. No false timeframe claims.
- **Quick example:** "The 5-minute safety check to run before giving any herb to your child."

## Formula Selection Guide

When choosing a formula, consider:

1. **Awareness level** -- Match the formula to where the reader currently sits on the Schwartz spectrum.
2. **Page type** -- Each formula works best on specific page types. See the "Page type" field above.
3. **Laws to emphasize** -- If the brief calls for high credibility, favor formulas tagged with Law 6. If urgency is needed, favor formulas tagged with Law 7.
4. **Archetype alignment** -- Each formula maps naturally to one or more of the 9 archetypes in WORKFLOW.md Section 3. Use the archetype table to verify compatibility.
5. **Compliance** -- All formulas must be filtered through Section 4 compliance rules before delivery. Formulas 11 (Shame Trigger), 15 (Imminent Threat), and 25 (Identity Shift) require extra compliance scrutiny for this brand.
